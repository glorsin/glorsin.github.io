Parts of the Eye

  • The iris is the colored part of the eye, and it controls the size of the pupil to let light into the eye.
  • Behind the colored iris sits a clear lens, which changes shape to focus light onto the retina so you can see.
  • While young the lens is soft and flexible, easily changing shape enabling the eye to focus on objects both close-up and far away.
  • The eye's tear film, cornea (protective outer layer) and lens bend light so it focuses on the retina. 70% of the focusing power comes from the cornea and 30% from the lens.
  • The retina receives the picture formed by these light rays. It sends the picture to the brain through the optic nerve, which is actually part of the brain.
  • The eye is mostly water (vitreous humor) with a harder outer shell, the sclera (white of eyeball).

Vitreous Humor

  • The vitreous humor is a clear gel-like substance that is composed mostly of water (98%) with some collagen, salt, and sugar.
  • It gives the eye its shape and form.
  • It is immobile, not served by any blood vessels, and is not actively regenerated or replenished.
  • The vitreous fills the space between the lens and the retina (80% of the volume of the eyeball) which lines the back of the eye and helps to keep the retina in place by pressing it against the choroid.
  • As the eye moves, the vitreous moves around on the retina without causing problems.
  • However, sometimes the vitreous may stick to the retina and pull hard enough to tear it causing fluid to pass through the tear and lift (detach) the retina. This is not painful, but a good indicator of the vitreous thinning is the presence of floaters.
  • Floaters are tiny clumps of cells or other material inside the vitreous appearing like small specks, lines, strings, cobwebs or clouds moving about your field of vision. As one ages, the vitreous starts to shrink and get thinner.
